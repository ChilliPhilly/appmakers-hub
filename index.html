Onboarding · Time tracking

Time tracking

Everything runs on tracked hours. It's how clients get billed and how you get paid. Get the folder and the tag right and the rest is easy.

Where it lives

ClickUp sidebar → Time Sheets. Use the ClickUp desktop app rather than the browser tab, it's more reliable. If the browser version shows the wrong thing, clear your cache. Two views: Timesheet (grid) and Time Entries (list, easier to read your notes and tags). Use whichever you prefer.

How to log an entry

  1. Hit Add Entry (better than typing inline, it captures everything).
  2. Type the project name, then filter to the TeamSpace Time Tracking folder so you don't pick the wrong item.
  3. Set the duration (e.g. 1 hour).
  4. Add a short note, what you did (e.g. "onboarding to ClickUp").
  5. Add one tag, QA or PM (see below).
  6. Leave Billable on (you'll basically never turn it off) and Save.

Which project do I track under?

Client work
Track under the specific project you worked on. This is almost always the answer.
Company time
Track under AppMakers MightyByte, but only for things like this onboarding, Thursday stand-ups, icebreakers and company updates.
The tag decides the billing. QA vs PM bill the client differently and only one tag is allowed. Pick based on what you did; if unsure, ask or make your best guess. Sometimes a PM marks QA deliberately to save the client hours.

The timer, if you prefer it

The Track Time icon in the top bar opens a timer you can start from anywhere in ClickUp. Press play, work, press stop and it saves automatically. Downside: you then have to go back and attach the task, note and tag. Sheer's tip: add those first so you're not editing afterwards.

Onboarding · Billing cutoffs

Billing cutoffs

Most clients are billed monthly; two or three big ones are billed twice a month. Cutoff dates stop people editing hours after a client's already been invoiced.

The month at a glance

1
30
4thbiweekly cutoff
19thmonthly cutoff
20thmost clients billed

The 4th, biweekly cutoff

Only affects the two or three big clients billed twice a month. It does not affect your end-of-month paycheck. Missed the 3rd or 4th? Just add those hours on the 5th, 6th or 7th. You still get paid and the client still gets billed.

The 19th, monthly cutoff

This is the one that matters. The 19th is the last day to add hours for that month; most clients are billed on the 20th. Forget the 18th/19th and add them on the 20th or 21st, and those hours won't appear until next month's paycheck. Crystal (Dan's assistant) messages everyone on the 18th/19th as a reminder and asks for your total hours.

Trying to add time before a passed cutoff auto-deletes it and sends you a Slack message saying so. The fix is always the same: log it on a date after the cutoff. Best defence: track daily.

Onboarding · Project lifecycle

Project lifecycle

How a project travels from a sales form to a shipping build. A lot of this runs on automations, so folders appear and archive on their own.

Phase 1 · R&D
  • Aaron · Sales
    Project added via a form, PM assigned. This creates the project folder with an R&D folder and a Project Documents folder inside.
  • Automation
    Client gets the first email with a link to a kickoff questionnaire. Their answers land in Project Documents; the PM gets a Slack ping.
  • PM
    Study the questionnaire before the kickoff call so you know what they want going in.
  • Developers
    Build estimates in the Estimates sheet once the PM shares designs. (Being moved into the web app over time.)
Phase 2 · Sign-off
  • Client
    Approves the estimates.
  • Aaron · Sales
    Sends the contract + first invoice via another form. Client signs and pays.
  • Automation
    R&D folder is archived and three new folders open: Bugs & Tasks, Builds, and Test Cases. Tasks are auto-pulled from the Estimates sheet with their hours.
Phase 3 · Build & test loop
  • Developers
    Build features, mark tasks Ready to test, and ship a build. See builds & testing →
  • QA, you
    Test the build, log bugs, mark things Done or Retest failed. Loop until it's clean.
  • Automation
    At the end, the liaison sends the client a review request.
Folder memory: Project Documents stays forever (kickoff links, estimates, designs, admin, QA and PM both add links here). R&D gets archived once the project moves into development.

How projects are scoped before a client signs

Before anything is signed, a project sits in R&D. The client fills the kickoff questionnaire (it lands in Project Documents), and developers build estimates from the designs in the Estimates sheet (moving into the web app). That package, the questionnaire, the designs, and the estimated QA/PM hours, is what the client approves. You can see it in ClickUp: the R&D folder holds the estimates and specs, and Project Documents holds the questionnaire and design links. Once signed, R&D is archived and Bugs & Tasks, Builds and Test Cases open.

Seeing progress, MVP scope and the schedule

Progress over time
The board statuses drive it, marking tasks Done nudges the project's progress bar. The Makers app and PM time-reporting dashboard show hours vs estimate.
What dev is on now
Tasks marked In progress. Queued work is To do / Not started; work ready for you is Ready to test.
The schedule
Shaped by sprints (each sprint is usually one build). See Running a project.
Which tasks are MVP
The tasks pulled from the estimates, grouped into the MVP phases (for Box and Ring, the four MVP phases in its R&D summary). If it is ever unclear what is MVP vs later, that is a fair question for the PM.

QA Job · Testing workflow

QA testing workflow

The full loop, from joining a project to signing off a build. You'll start here as a QA shadowing a PM.

1. Get onto the project

The VA assigns you. Usually the lead developer or PM gets on a call to onboard you, walking the designs or explaining the app and what's expected. On small projects they may just ask you to onboard yourself.

2. Get your bearings before testing

Review the specs (in ClickUp or the channel), the designs, and the estimates (how many QA hours were quoted and every task we said we'd deliver). Then build a test plan. The first build is usually thin, just login and project setup.

3. The build loop

  1. A new build is marked Testing, you get a Slack ping. Open Bugs & Tasks, filter to Ready to test.
  2. Test each task. Works? Mark Done. Broken? Mark Retest failed or log a new bug.
  3. When you've been through everything, go to Builds and mark the build Complete. See builds & merging.
How deep to test each build? Test everything on the first build. Later builds, do a quick pass plus whatever the release notes say changed. If notes are thin, just ask the dev on the channel: "would anything have broken with this release?" You don't need a full deep-dive every time.
Real-world example

On a first build you might only see "Apple login", "Google login" and "project setup". You open the build, log in both ways, and if the app opens at all you mark project setup Done. Anything that doesn't work becomes a bug.

QA Job · Finding & writing bugs

Finding & writing bugs

You test the ready-to-test tasks, mark the ones that work Done, and log anything broken as a bug. A clear write-up is the difference between a fast fix and a confused developer.

Reading the board

Green tick
Done. Tested and working (you set this).
Orange
Ready to test. The dev has released it, your cue to test.
To do
You've flagged it for the dev to work on next.
Not started
The default when a task is created.

If a ready-to-test task works, mark it Done and move on. Obvious ones (Apple login, project setup) need no description; if you can open the app at all, setup is done.

The fields that matter

  1. Name it clearly as a bug so the developer sees at a glance what it is.
  2. Set it to To do and assign a developer (if you're not sure which, assign both and they'll sort it out, but always assign someone).
  3. Open Show custom fields and mark Bug or Feature.
  4. Attach screenshots / video, set the platform (iOS, Android, web, iPad, all, or leave blank), and a priority (default P0).

Write the bug like this

The format the team aims for. Paste rough notes into Claude or ChatGPT to shape them into this. If you attach a video, add PFA ("please find attached") to the description so the dev knows to look.

# Title, starts with where + what Bug: Admin, job seeker list resets Description: The saved job-seeker list clears after refresh. PFA video. Expected behaviour: The list stays after a refresh. Steps to reproduce: 1) Open admin 2) Save a list 3) Refresh Expected result: List is still there. Actual result: List is empty.

Bug vs Feature

Bug
Something's broken or behaving wrong.
Feature
A client request or a missing piece you're adding, not a defect. E.g. the dev never added the little "show password" eye on login, that's a feature task, not a bug.

Retest failed vs a new bug

Retest failed
The dev marked a bug Ready to test (said they fixed it), you tested, and it's still broken. Mark the same task Retest failed and tell them.
New bug
Something different is now broken. Log a fresh bug.
Splitting example: if "Login" is one task but only Apple login is broken (Google works), mark Login Done and raise a new "Apple login doesn't work" bug, so the two don't get tangled.

Priority

Early on, post bugs to Slack first and let the PM set the priority. You'll pick up the feel for it quickly.

P0
App-breaking. Stops people using it.
P1
Serious, but not a full blocker.
P2
Minor / lower urgency.
Make the board yours. Build your own QA-list view. Pin it, make it private, protect it (reverts other people's changes) and set it to auto-save. List, board or Kanban all work. You can hide closed tasks so you only see To do / In progress / Ready to test.
QA Job · Test cases & plans

Test cases & plans

Every project has a Test Cases folder in ClickUp. It starts empty, you build the plan.

Build a test plan

Before you test, add cases with these fields:

Name
What you're testing (e.g. "Email login").
Steps
How to run the test.
Expected result
What should happen.
Status
Pass, fail, standby (revisit later), or none.
Note
Anything extra, e.g. "still works on build 0.03".

Start faster

Common cases repeat across apps, splash screen, login, sign up, log out, onboarding. Copy them from a similar project's Test Cases folder rather than retyping, and lean on Claude/ChatGPT to draft cases. Add edge cases as they occur to you while testing. (Auto-populating a few defaults is something Sheer may set up with Miguel.)

Re-running basics: you don't formally re-run every basic case on each build. Do a quick check, then mark standby or drop a note so you know it was re-checked.
Real-world example

Name: Email login. Steps: 1) Open app 2) Enter valid email + password 3) Tap Login. Expected: Lands on the home screen. Status: Pass / Fail / Standby.

QA Job · Builds & merging

Builds & merging

The Builds folder is how a developer hands a testable build to you. Used by devs, QAs and PMs.

How a build reaches you

  1. Developer marks completed tasks Ready to test in Bugs & Tasks.
  2. An automation links those tasks into a build (with their IDs and names).
  3. Developer opens the build, adds a version name, release notes (some devs write lots, some none), and an APK, then marks it Testing.
  4. A Slack notification goes to the PM and QA with the release notes and APK, so it's in two places.

How you test it

Two ways in: go to Bugs & Tasks filtered to Ready to test (and Retest failed), or open the build under Builds and use Link tasks to see everything attached. Work through each task, marking Done or Retest failed and logging new bugs. See test devices & tools for how to install a build.

Ready to release & merging

With two developers (say back-end and front-end), one may finish work the other hasn't. Pushing it alone would give you nothing to see, so they mark it Ready to release (blue): "done, but not in this build, it'll come once we merge." Devs merge into one build, so you shouldn't normally see two builds testing at once.

Mark it complete

When you've been through the whole build, go to Builds and mark it Complete, this is the QA's job. That fires a Slack message telling the PM and devs the build is tested. If you ever see several builds "testing" at once, it usually means a build wasn't marked complete, or a dev pushed a quick hotfix mid-test. Finish your current build, then move to the next (or if it's just a one-change hotfix, mark the current done and focus on the changed area).

Sprints and builds: each sprint is usually one build. Bugs you add don't always show inside the sprint (that's the PM's call), but it's good practice to add the important ones to the sprint so the dev's direction is clear.
Coming soon (Lobby's process): a per-build summary listing new bugs (P0/P1) with their ID + name, any retest-failed, and QA notes. The goal is to auto-post it to the channel when you move a build from Testing to Done. For now it's filled in manually.

QA Job · Sandbox testing

Sandbox testing — in-app purchases & subscriptions

How to test paid features and subscriptions safely. No real money is ever charged. Includes the full one-time iOS setup so you can clear purchase history and control renewal speed.

TestFlight vs dev build — read first. The sandbox-tester flow below (with Clear Purchase History) needs a dev-signed build (Xcode / Ad-Hoc). TestFlight builds use your real Apple ID and their purchase history can’t be cleared. To reset a TestFlight subscription instead: cancel it in Settings → [your name] → Subscriptions and let it lapse, or ask the dev for a dev-signed build.

Quick reference

iOS — full setup (dev-signed build)

One-time setup to run true sandbox purchases you can reset. Do these in order — Developer Mode only appears after the phone has been connected to Xcode.

1. Install Xcode
Mac App Store → search Xcode → Install (~10–15 GB). Open it once, accept the licence, let it install extra components. Xcode → Settings → Accounts → add your Apple ID.
2. Create a sandbox tester
App Store Connect → Users and Access → Sandbox → Test Accounts → +. Use a unique email that isn’t already an Apple ID (a +alias works). Don’t sign it into iCloud. No access? Ask the dev/Sheer for credentials.
3. Connect the iPhone
Plug in via cable → on the phone tap Trust This Computer → passcode. In Xcode: Window → Devices and Simulators → wait for “preparing device for development” to finish.
4. Enable Developer Mode
Settings → Privacy & Security → Developer Mode → On → restart → confirm. (Only appears after step 3.)
5. Install the dev-signed build
From a .ipa: Xcode → Devices and Simulators → your iPhone → Installed Apps → + → pick the file. From the project: open it, pick your iPhone, set a Team in Signing & Capabilities, press ▶ Run. Must be development/Ad-Hoc, not TestFlight.
6. Sign into the sandbox account
Settings → Developer → scroll to Sandbox Apple Account → Sign In → enter the sandbox tester (not your real Apple ID).
7. Run the purchase
Open the app → paywall → Subscribe/Buy → the sheet shows [Environment: Sandbox] → confirm. No real charge; renewals run on an accelerated clock.
8. Reset between runs
Clear purchase history: Settings → Developer → Sandbox Apple Account → Manage → Clear Purchase History (or App Store Connect → Sandbox → tester). Renewal speed: App Store Connect → Sandbox → tester (default fastest). Each tester allows ~12 renewals then auto-expires — make a new one for a clean slate.

Troubleshooting

No “Developer” in Settings
Developer Mode isn’t on yet — do step 3 (connect to Xcode) then step 4. The toggle won’t appear from a TestFlight-only install.
No “Sandbox Apple Account” section
It appears under Settings → Developer once Developer Mode is on. If purchases still use your real ID, you’re on a TestFlight build — get a dev-signed one.
Purchase silently fails
Check: dev-signed build, signed into the sandbox account (not real ID), and the IAP products are approved / “Ready to Submit” in App Store Connect.
QA Job · Mentor insights

QA insights from Carlos

From a 1:1 with Carlos Franco (8 Jul 2026), ex QA/PM, now full-time QA on Maxim. How an experienced MightyByte QA actually runs a project, beyond the formal process. Watch the recording (76 min).

The order of work: explore first, then write cases

Do exploratory testing before writing test cases. Go through every nook and cranny on the fly to build familiarity with the app, then write the cases that occur to you: positive, negative and boundary. You can pre-write cases from the specs before the first build if you want a head start, but Carlos prefers to explore first. And remember who reads what: devs read bugs, not test cases. Test cases are your coverage tool; the bug report is the communication tool, so bugs get the full detail every time.

When do you stop testing?

There is no concrete rule. You stop when you're confident you've covered every feature with positive, negative and boundary cases and you've tried everything you can think of. Coverage confidence, not a checklist, is the exit condition.

Regression strategy: sanity per sprint, full suite before release

New feature lands
Deep-test that feature (new cases, every nook and cranny). A new feature usually breaks something else.
Rest of the app
Run a sanity test: a condensed pass over the core features only, not the full suite.
Before store release
Run full regression, the whole suite. A mature project can be 200+ cases.

Priority feel (in practice it's P0 or P1)

P0
Broken on normal, obvious usage: a page that errors when you tap Subscription or Logout. Blockers.
P1
A real bug on a hidden path most users won't hit, e.g. tapping return on an empty search field throws an exception page.
P2
Typos, a missing period. Carlos has almost never used it.

One bug, one report, across builds

If a bug isn't fixed by the next build, don't log it again. The original report stands; add a note like "still present on build 1.0.3" and remind the dev. And always log bugs in the system even if you also discussed them on a call, because the dev may take days to get to the fix and shouldn't have to remember the conversation.

Evidence workflow

Don't screen-record your whole session, you could be exploring for 20 to 30 minutes at a time. Explore freely; when you find a bug, reproduce it once more with the native screen recorder running, then attach that video to the report. iOS and Android both have built-in recorders.

UI judgement: Figma is the referee

Think the UI looks off? Check it against Figma. If it doesn't match, raise it with the dev (a bug report plus a ping). If it matches but you still don't like it, that's the client's chosen design, out of scope unless they ask for a change. Watch for screen-size differences: an element sitting lower on a smaller phone may not be a real bug. And missing functionality you feel should exist is a feature request, not a bug, and needs the client to want it.

Odds and ends worth knowing

Android IAP
Sandbox subscription payments error on virtually every project. Report it to the dev, then keep testing on the free plan.
Devices
Don't buy your own Android; ask Dovrat for one. BrowserStack in the meantime.
Builds
Always test the latest build. APKs arrive on the ClickUp Builds page or Slack (depends on the dev's habit); iOS arrives as a TestFlight invite when the dev is ready for you.
Automation
Small projects have none (budget); devs may keep unit tests on their side, ask them. Playwright is starting on Maxim but nobody has had time, an opening if you want it.
QA hours
In estimates, QA gets roughly a fifth of the engineering hours (Carlos's info is about 18 months old).
AI tricks Carlos rates: paste the client-call transcript into AI next to your draft spec and ask for discrepancies ("you wrote X, but the client actually said Y"). PM Job · Phase 1 R&D

Kickoff call to specs

Two jobs in one motion: run the kickoff call, then turn its transcript into an approved spec. The spec is what the designer builds from, so a missed or invented feature here is a wrong estimate later.

Your tasks

  1. Run the kickoff call. Work through your prepared questions, as specifically as possible. Make sure the call is recorded in Fathom. One call is usually enough; a complex project may need two or three.
  2. Park out-of-scope ideas as future features. If the client drifts out of scope mid-call, never say no. Say "great idea, I'll note it as a future feature". It lands in the Future Features section and becomes a paid phase-two conversation near project end.
  3. Pull the transcript from Fathom after the call.
  4. Generate the spec section by section. Start a fresh LLM chat, paste the prompt below, then the transcript. It returns one section at a time and waits for your approval.
  5. Interrogate every section before approving it. Check each claim against what the client actually said. Labi's live example: the AI invented "fast authentication", a feature from nowhere. A made-up feature or a missed feature both cost you; the client either complains about it or asks for it, and the estimate is wrong either way.
  6. Say "go ahead" for the full document once every section is approved.
  7. Sanity-check the whole spec against what a good spec contains. The gaps become questions for the client.

What it produces

Overview
What you are building, immediate objectives, the primary product goal, MVP direction and product principles.
Core Features
Bullet list of the MVP features.
Core Features & Screens
Each screen broken into purpose, core elements and notes, so the designer can build from it.
Branding Guidelines
Any brand direction pulled from the call.
Future Features
Everything parked as Post-MVP. Your phase-two pipeline.

The prompt (copy this)

Paste this verbatim, then the transcript underneath it.

You are "R&D Assistant," a product R&D strategist working for Labinot Krasniqi. Your job is to turn raw client transcripts into structured R&D documents for MVP development. Work in small steps, finish one section at a time and ask Labinot for approval before continuing. Ask follow-up questions if anything in the transcript is unclear or requires assumptions. When Labinot pastes a transcript, analyze it carefully and extract only product-relevant information. Ignore chit-chat, small talk, and vague ideas. Use this structure: Overview Core Features (bullet list) Core Features & Screens Branding Guidelines Future Features, Post MVP Rules: Always ask for approval before moving to the next section Ask follow-up questions when transcript details are vague Be fast but precise, you are evaluated on clarity and accuracy Use short, crisp sentences and bullet points throughout Never output the full document unless Labinot says "go ahead" Always begin your first response with: "Here's the first section: [OVERVIEW], let me know when to continue or if you'd like changes."

Watch-outs

  1. Never let it dump the whole document. Nobody properly reviews a three-page doc. Section by section is how you actually catch mistakes.
  2. Match the tool to the client. Simple builds go through the prompt; a genuinely complex project may still be written by hand. Labi finds ChatGPT stronger for planning and documents, Claude stronger for building. Use what works for you.
  3. Everything is MVP-oriented. If it is not needed to validate the product, it goes to Future Features, not the spec.

Done when

Every section is approved, checked against what the client actually said, and the spec is ready for the designer. Next: Designs: direction to build.

Expected hours

Kickoff call plus spec creation together: about 3 to 4 hours. Conservative on purpose; budgets are tight and R&D does not bill like development does.

PM Job · Reading & writing specs

Reading & writing specs

A spec is the shared source of truth, so design, dev and QA all build the same thing. As QA you read it to learn what "correct" means; as you move toward PM you'll write them. Box and Ring is your worked example.

Anatomy of a good feature spec

Seven parts. If any are missing, that's your first set of questions.

1 Context
The problem, the goal, and who it's for. Why are we building this?
2 Flow
Entry point → each step → the end states. A user story, not a feature list.
3 States & edges
Empty, loading, error, offline, concurrent, declined, timed-out. The unhappy paths.
4 Acceptance criteria
Testable statements of "done". This is what you convert straight into test cases.
5 Non-functional
Performance, security, privacy, accessibility. The numbers and limits.
6 Out of scope
What this is NOT doing, and what's deferred past MVP.
7 Open questions
Unknowns and dependencies, each with an owner and a name attached.

How the Box and Ring doc measures up

Strong on
Flows and UX detail, and the product thinking (the scoring philosophy is genuinely good, it explains the why).
Thin on
Acceptance criteria, non-functional requirements, and a clear edge-case matrix per feature. Great to learn structure from, then layer the missing parts on.
The QA-to-spec loop. Every vague line in a spec is either a test case waiting to be written or a question to ask. Reading a spec as a QA is practising to write one: you spot what's ambiguous, and that's exactly what you'd make explicit if you were the author.

Spec smells (red flags to catch)

  • Vague verbs: "handles", "supports", "manages", with no detail on how.
  • Missing numbers: limits, timeouts, grace periods, sizes left unstated.
  • No error or empty states, only the happy path is described.
  • A decision with no owner ("TBD" with nobody's name on it).
  • No definition of done, so nobody can agree when it's finished.
  • Duplicated or clashing section numbers (a sign it grew without a tidy-up).

A template you can copy

Feature: [name] Context: problem, goal, who it's for Flow: entry point → steps → end states States & edges: empty / error / offline / concurrent / declined Acceptance criteria: - [ ] testable statement of done - [ ] ... Non-functional: performance, security, privacy, accessibility Out of scope: what this is NOT / deferred Open questions: unknown → owner

PM Job · Phase 1 R&D

Internal design review

The gate before the client ever sees the designs. "If this gets approved, this gets built. The estimations are from this. One missed flow, and you're over budget." Expect about an hour, done in three passes.

Your tasks

  1. Re-read the spec first. It is the source of truth you are checking against. Labi reviews from memory because he wrote it; until you have that context, put the spec next to the designs.
  2. Pass 1, scan. Go top to bottom, screen by screen, and journal what views exist. No judgments yet, so you do not jump to conclusions.
  3. Pass 2, flows. Run the flows checklist below. Breadth before depth: confirm every key flow and core feature exists before you critique anything.
  4. Pass 3, detail. Run the detail checklist below, screen by screen.
  5. Classify anything not in the spec with the out-of-scope decision tree below: remove it, or hold it as an upsell for the client call.
  6. Comment in Figma as you go. Press C and click to comment. Tag or CC the designer on anything needing action. Keep it tactful; "what app did we reference for this view?" beats "this looks wrong".
  7. Do your offline research on anything you could not place, and post the remaining comments over the following hours.
  8. Re-review after the designer's changes. When a pass comes back clean, book the client design review.

Pass 2 · The flows checklist

Auth
Can I log in? Can I sign up? Are the sign-up methods the ones the client asked for (Google, Apple, email)? Forgot password? Verification code on email and password? Resend code? Missing auth flows change the estimate, which is why they come first.
Core features
Can I use each core feature the client asked for? (Glitter-verse: brush by hand, add effects.)
Basic functions
Create a new project? Import media? Video sample or preview where one is expected?
User journeys
New user vs returning user flows both exist. Onboarding screens communicate what the client wanted.

Pass 3 · The detail checklist

Out of scope
Anything in the designs that is not in the spec. The biggest catch; classify it with the decision tree.
Colours, text, spacing
Plus functionality and workflows on top.
Naming & consistency
Same thing, two names ("portal" vs "library" vs "project"). Profile and settings split apart when they usually live together.
Navigation logic
The nav bar should be coherent, not padded with items just to fill slots.
Duplicates & ambiguity
Two sliders both called "intensity". Effects in two places (brush vs mask). Rename or clarify.
Element separation
Controls in odd places, like "original audio" as a standalone setting instead of inside the player. Point at an app that does it well.
Overlays & states
Popups and walkthroughs dim the app behind them properly.
Dynamic vs static
Reason about real estate: panels that collapse after selection can be right, static ones can eat more space. Think about what the user wants mid-action.
Screen consistency
Similar screens behave alike (edit vs eraser page heights), and differences have a reason.
Unexplained elements
Anything you cannot place ("auto-track"): flag it, ask the designer where it came from.
Benchmarks
Compare against apps that already work (CapCut). Do not reinvent the wheel.

The out-of-scope decision tree

  1. Nonsensical or clearly out of scope → tell the designer to remove it.
  2. Makes sense but was never in the spec → remove it, or hold it as an upsell for the client call: "we didn't discuss this, but it looked really cool and it's here as a placeholder. We can totally remove it, but estimations are modular so we can scope it in, and scope it out of version one if you'd rather. What do you think?" Then leave the choice to them.
  3. Budget-conscious clients will usually decline big additions; float it anyway, the transparency itself builds trust. Be proactive and transparent about budget spending all the way; clients appreciate it and most of the time they approve.

Boundaries

  1. You are the backline, not the designer. Pixel spacing and polish are theirs. You check correctness against the spec.
  2. Naming is mostly the client's call. Suggest if something jumps out; otherwise leave it.
  3. Do not edit the Figma. An edit account exists, but designs are built from components and touching one breaks more than you expect. Tiny colour or text fixes only, when the designer is busy.
  4. Defer nice-to-haves (mute vs remove audio) as phase-two rather than forcing them in now.

Expected hours

About 1 hour, deliberately conservative; budget for the possibility of a second pass after the designer's changes. Log it against the project.

Real-world example

On Glitter-verse the designer added a "Discover Effects" screen full of effects. The spec said two to three effect combinations, total. Labi's call: 100% out of scope, but it makes sense as a feature, so instead of deleting it he holds it for the client call as a modular, optional scope-in. Spot it, classify it, turn it into a removal or an upsell. That single catch is the job.

PM Job · Maintenance plans

Maintenance plans

How to explain, sell and run the post-launch maintenance retainer. Straight from Aaron, with the source doc at the bottom.

What maintenance is

Maintenance is a subscription, not R&D credits. Clients pay for responsiveness and continuity, not a pool of hours they can later reclaim. There is no credit roll-back if they "didn't use it much."

How to position it to clients

  • It's an industry-standard retainer for an existing production app.
  • It keeps the original team familiar with the codebase involved, so there's no onboarding delay.
  • The key benefit is speed and priority: the team is expected to respond within an agreed timeframe, often 1 to 2 business days depending on the arrangement.

What it covers

  • Bug fixes
  • Minor tweaks / feature adjustments
  • Content updates
  • Keeping the app smooth and supported over time

If the client doesn't want maintenance

That's okay. Explain that they can work a la carte / ad hoc, but:

  • They'll go into a queue.
  • Fathom / AppMakers can't guarantee who will handle it.
  • And can't guarantee when it will be addressed.

When it makes sense financially

Aaron's rough rule of thumb: the retainer works when it's about 10 to 30% of the client's monthly recurring revenue. His example: if a client makes $10k/month, a $2.5k/month retainer can make sense.

What it's for operationally

Covered by maintenance
Small ongoing changes, without needing a whole new R&D cycle.
New R&D instead
If the client wants something new and substantial, that likely becomes new R&D.

Who should sell it

Aaron said it usually feels more natural coming from the PM who already owns the relationship. He's happy to join calls if needed, but it's usually better if the PM explains it. The pitch, in three lines:

"We've delivered V2 / the current work." "Maintenance is how we continue supporting you." "It keeps the team available and responsive."
Overages and extra work. If the logged work goes well beyond the retainer, flag it to Aaron or Dan. They decide case by case whether to invoice overages, absorb some cost, or move the client to a higher tier.

Projects · Box and Ring

Box and Ring

A couples' app for having a structured, refereed argument. Here's the whole thing in one read: what it is, how a fight works, what's actually being built, and where you'd push as QA. The full 15-section spec and the Figma are the detail behind it.

How to read this. This is the testing playbook and your QA training ground for Box and Ring: which parts need normal QA, which parts need AI evaluation, and the handful of invariants that outrank every other bug. It sits on top of the general QA workflow, bug write-up and test-case process, it does not replace them.

Start here: your first pass

  1. Read this whole page once, then skim the R&D summary so you know what the app is meant to do.
  2. Build your test-case library from the privacy matrix, the invariants, and the scoring rules below.
  3. Start your golden test conversations (see AI evals) early, they are reusable on every build.
  4. On each build: run the invariants and safety checks first, then the feature you were told changed.

Your job here is two questions, not one

Every feature gets tested against two things, in order. First, "does it work?" (the button fires, the timer counts down, nothing crashes). That is normal QA, and it is the floor. Second, "does it do what it's for?" (a couple finishes a spar feeling closer and fairly treated, Glovy feels supportive not cold). On this app that second question is not a nice-to-have layered on top, it is the requirement. A build can be 100% bug-free and still fail, if it makes a couple feel judged or lets one partner beat the other. When two issues compete for your attention, the one that breaks the vision wins.

Two kinds of testing live in this app

Deterministic QA
Same input, same output, pass/fail. The timer, whisper privacy plumbing, round transitions, notifications, login, scheduling gates. Test these the normal way.
AI evaluation
Everything Glovy says or scores. The AI can give a different answer to the same input, so there is no single correct result to assert. You judge outputs against criteria (safe, on-tone, neutral, accurate, no invention), not against one right answer. Realistically about half of this job is AI evals, and it is the harder half.
AI is not deterministic. Feed Glovy the same conversation twice and the score can differ slightly. Do not test it for an exact number. Test it for reasonableness (is this a defensible call?) and consistency within a range (does it land in roughly the same band each time?).

The invariants that outrank every other bug

If any of these break, it is not a normal bug, it is a stop-ship. Test them exhaustively and adversarially.

Never out-score
No shared screen may ever let one partner see they beat the other. The couple is the team, the conflict is the opponent. Individual feedback lives only in the private "Your Corner", measured against your own past, never your partner's. A comparison leaking onto a shared screen is critical.
Private never leaks
A whisper meant for one partner, the challenger's private note, and "Your Corner" must never surface to the other partner. One leak destroys trust in the whole product.
Safety always reachable
Emergency exit and timeout are visible throughout a spar, crisis resources are always accessible, and out-of-scope situations (abuse, self-harm, addiction) escalate to real help. Every safety event is logged.

AI evals: how to test what Glovy does

Build a library of golden test conversations (deliberately crafted transcripts: a calm one, a heated one, a contemptuous one, one that mentions self-harm, a very short one). Run each through Glovy and rate the output against a rubric. Re-run the same set on every build to catch regressions (Glovy getting worse after a change). That library becomes one of your most valuable assets on this project.

Two AI failures to hunt specifically. Hallucination: Glovy must "show its work", every rating and summary line ties to a real moment in the chat. A quote or claim that was never said is a bug. Cross-couple leakage: when memory / RAG arrives, retrieving one couple's history into another couple's spar is a catastrophic privacy failure. Test for it deliberately.

Scoring rules you CAN pass/fail test

The scorecard is mostly AI judgment, but the designers laid hard rules on top of it. Those rules are deterministic, so you can assert them like normal QA.

Contempt caps Tone
Feed a transcript with one clearly contemptuous line (mockery, name-calling). Tone must not rise above its low ceiling no matter how pleasant the rest was.
Too short = No Decision
A four-message exchange must return "No Decision", never a Knockout. Not enough conversation to judge fairly.
Baseline is not zero
Showing up and engaging honestly earns a passing mark. A score only drops below that for a real reason.
Safety overrides the score
If anything raises a safety concern, the scorecard steps aside entirely and support resources appear instead. Never a number over someone's wellbeing.

Real-time text, where the nasty bugs live

The live fight runs in a Durable Object (the "BoutRoom") over WebSocket, so two phones have to agree on message order, the current round, the timer and pause state. Race conditions and reconnection are the classic failure points. Throw adversarial cases at it:

  1. Kill wifi mid-round on one device, then bring it back, do the missed messages arrive, in order, with the timer still correct?
  2. Background the app during a round, does presence update, does the round state survive on return?
  3. Both partners type and send at the exact same moment, do messages scramble or duplicate?
  4. Pause on one device, resume from the other, do both land in the same state?
  5. Let a round timer expire while someone is mid-message, what happens to that message and the transition?

What it is

Two partners work through one conflict as a guided, timed conversation dressed up as a boxing match. An AI character, Glovy, referees and coaches. It is not therapy, and it hands people to real professionals the moment things get serious (abuse, self-harm, addiction).

The whole MVP exists to answer one question: "Can two people successfully complete a guided fight, and feel it was valuable?" Anything that doesn't serve that is deferred. When you're weighing a bug or a question, that's the bar it's measured against.

How one fight works

  1. A partner starts a challenge: picks how they feel (1 of 12), a topic, and a goal, plus an optional private note only Glovy sees.
  2. The other partner accepts (in the MVP it's accept or decline, no haggling) and does their own emotional check-in.
  3. Both agree to the ground rules (a hard gate, no agreement = no fight), then a 3-2-1 countdown.
  4. Four timed rounds of real-time text: Tell → Mirror → Propose → Commit (3 / 2 / 3 / 2 min), refereed by Glovy, with private whispers to Glovy on the side.
  5. It ends and Glovy delivers a summary, and later a full scorecard, on how the couple did against the conflict.

The words you'll keep hearing

Spar / Fight / Bout
One structured conversation about one issue.
Glovy
The AI referee and coach. Runs the fight, whispers advice, scores it.

Tell → Mirror → Propose → Commit (timed 3 / 2 / 3 / 2 min).

A private message between one partner and Glovy. The other partner never sees it.

What's being built, and when

The MVP ships in four phases over roughly 14 weeks, each with a hard "done when" gate. This is your working model; the full spec is the reference behind it.

  • Phase 1 · Weeks 1 to 3
    Foundation & pairing. Accounts, chat-style onboarding, avatar, invite + pair, dashboard shell, light/dark.
    Done when: both partners land on a shared dashboard, on iOS and Android, in light and dark.
  • Phase 2 · Weeks 3 to 7
    The core fight loop (text). Scheduling, ground-rules gate, real-time text fight over 4 rounds, basic public Glovy, an AI summary, pause/resume.
    Done when: two people complete a full timed, multi-round, refereed text fight that ends in a summary. This is the primary proof.
  • Phase 3 · Weeks 7 to 10
    Make it valuable & safe. The whisper system, trigger detection, tap-to-use suggestions, the full 5-dimension scorecard, the full safety net, and shadow boxing.

  • Phase 4 · Weeks 10 to 14
    Polish, insight & pilot ship. History & stats, no-show/rematch, notifications, Glovy animation, minimal admin, legal, app-store prep.
    Done when: a polished, legally-covered build is in pilot couples' hands.
The scoring idea (worth understanding). The couple is the team; the opponent is the conflict, never each other. Golden rule: no shared screen ever lets one partner out-score the other. It's grounded in Gottman research, shown as a boxing decision (Knockout, Unanimous, Split, Draw, Saved by the Bell) with private per-person coaching underneath.

Wave

A document wallet for maritime professionals: a safe deposit box for the 60–70 certificates and licences a seafarer carries through their career. Scan, tag, store, retrieve, export and print — and critically, view everything fully offline. iOS + Android app with a simple web admin.

The one invariant that outranks everything. The app must show every uploaded document with zero connectivity. A seafarer mid-ocean or in a foreign port with no signal still needs to open, view and export their certificates. If offline viewing breaks, nothing else matters — test it first, adversarially, on every build.

What we're building

A wallet app for mariners who travel the world and must carry their professional documents and licences everywhere. Certifications are extensive and critical: without the right valid papers a seafarer cannot trade, operate or work. Wave digitises that bundle into an electronic safe deposit box — store documents, retrieve them easily, export and transact with them, and reprint if the originals are lost. Users scan all their documentation and tag each item under a specific category / folder. The look and feel target is Apple Wallet, with scan/upload into folders.
